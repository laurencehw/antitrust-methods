## What to read next

The field moves quickly. The citations below point to work that extends or complements the methods in this book, organized by theme.

**Causal inference and econometric methods.** For the latest on staggered difference-in-differences, see @goodman_bacon_2021 and @sun_abraham_2021, which diagnose the biases in standard two-way fixed effects estimators when treatment timing varies across units. @callaway_santanna_2021 proposes an alternative estimator that avoids these pitfalls. For synthetic control extensions, @athey_bayati_imbens_2021 develop matrix completion methods that generalize the synthetic control framework to multiple treated units and time-varying treatment.

**Digital platform competition.** The Stigler Committee on Digital Platforms (@stigler_committee_2019) provided the foundational analysis that shaped subsequent regulatory proposals, including the EU's Digital Markets Act. For empirical work on platform market power, see @crawford_etal_2022 on search bias measurement and @johnson_etal_2023 on the competitive effects of default positions. The intersection of AI and antitrust is addressed by @calvano_vijay_2023, who show that pricing algorithms can sustain supra-competitive outcomes without explicit communication.

**Labor market power.** @azab_etal_2024 survey the rapidly growing empirical literature on labor monopsony, covering concentration measurement, supply elasticity estimation, and the wage effects of noncompete enforcement. For policy analysis, @naidu_posner_weyl_2018 remain the essential reference on the institutional design of labor antitrust enforcement.

**Merger retrospectives and empirical IO.** @weinberg_hosken_2013 provide a comprehensive survey of merger retrospective studies, while @miller_weinberg_2017 demonstrate how structural models can be validated against post-merger price data. For the latest on differentiated-products merger simulation, @conlon_gortmaker_2020 update the PyBLP framework for modern computational environments.

## What we have built

This book began from a simple observation: antitrust analysis is at its best when it integrates three distinct streams of evidence --- quantitative estimation, documentary context, and expert judgment --- and deploys them across the institutional settings where competition policy actually operates. We called this the **evidence triad**, and it has structured every chapter from market definition through litigation practice. The triad is not a theoretical innovation; it is a description of how successful cases are actually built. No single method carries the day. Econometric estimates of overcharge are persuasive when they align with internal documents discussing pricing strategy. Merger simulation results gain credibility when they are consistent with customer testimony about switching costs. Screens for bid rotation become actionable when they coincide with leniency statements identifying the participants. The chapters in this book have shown how to construct each leg of the triad and how to weave them together into a coherent evidentiary package.

The tri-jurisdictional scope --- United States, European Union, and South Africa --- has been equally central to the book's design. These are not three versions of the same regime. They differ in their legal standards (per se versus rule of reason, effects-based versus form-based approaches), their institutional architectures (dual federal agencies versus a single Commission versus a Commission-Tribunal model), their remedial traditions (consent decrees versus commitment decisions versus public-interest conditions), and the data environments in which practitioners operate. The US system offers the richest case law and the most developed econometric tradition. The EU provides the most sophisticated framework for digital markets and the clearest articulation of the effects-based approach. South Africa demonstrates how competition policy can address structural inequality, employment effects, and small-business participation alongside conventional consumer-welfare analysis. A practitioner who can navigate all three --- or at least understand how methods translate across them --- brings a perspective that is increasingly valuable in a world of cross-border mergers, global platforms, and converging enforcement priorities.

## The evidence triad in practice

To see how the triad works in practice, consider a hypothetical matter that draws on methods from across the book. A competition authority receives a complaint that a series of hospital mergers in a particular region has led to higher commercial insurance prices and suppressed wages for nursing staff. The analysis proceeds in stages:

**Market definition** ([Chapter 3: Market Definition](03-market-definition.md)) establishes the relevant product and geographic boundaries. For the product market, critical loss analysis and diversion ratios derived from patient-flow data determine whether inpatient general acute care constitutes a distinct market or whether outpatient substitutes constrain pricing. For the geographic market, patient origin data --- where patients actually travel for care --- defines the relevant area, often much narrower than the national or even state-level market that merging parties might prefer. In labor markets, commuting zones and occupation codes define the boundaries within which employers compete for workers.

**Quantitative analysis** draws on the causal toolkit from [Chapter 2: Research Design](02-research-design.md). A difference-in-differences design compares price and wage trajectories in markets where mergers occurred against matched control markets, with pre-trend equivalence tests and placebo exercises to validate the identification. If the mergers were staggered across time and geography, modern estimators --- @callaway_santanna_2021 or @sun_abraham_2021 --- address the biases that afflict standard two-way fixed effects. For single-merger cases, synthetic control methods (@abadie_diamond_hainmueller_2010) construct a counterfactual from a weighted combination of untreated markets. The IO toolkit from [Chapter 4: IO Toolkit](04-io-toolkit.md) provides demand estimation and merger simulation to assess prospective competitive effects.

**Documentary evidence** grounds the quantitative findings. Internal board memoranda discussing "pricing discipline" or "wage benchmarking" against competitor hospitals provide context for the estimated effects. Discovery documents may reveal capacity decisions, network negotiations with insurers, or HR policies that suppressed worker mobility. In the South African context, the public-interest mandate of the Competition Act requires additional attention to employment effects, small-business participation, and the impact on historically disadvantaged persons --- considerations that generate their own documentary record.

**Expert judgment** ties the analysis to legal standards. In the US, the question is whether the mergers "may be substantially to lessen competition" under Section 7 of the Clayton Act, evaluated against the 2023 Merger Guidelines. In the EU, the test is whether the transaction would "significantly impede effective competition," particularly through non-coordinated effects. In South Africa, the Tribunal must weigh competitive effects alongside public-interest factors, and remedies may include conditions that would be unthinkable in US or EU practice --- requirements to maintain employment levels, support small suppliers, or divest to historically disadvantaged buyers.

This is the workflow that the book has been building toward. Each chapter has contributed a piece: the market definition tools, the causal inference methods, the IO demand estimation, the cartel detection screens, the merger simulation techniques, the qualitative research protocols, the litigation and damages frameworks. The practitioner's task is to select the right combination for the matter at hand and to present the result in a form that regulators, courts, and opposing experts can evaluate.

## Emerging frontiers

The field is not standing still. Three developments in particular deserve attention from practitioners who want to stay current.

**Artificial intelligence and algorithmic competition.** The rise of AI in pricing, procurement, and market allocation poses novel challenges for antitrust analysis. Pricing algorithms that learn to coordinate tacitly --- without explicit communication --- test the boundaries of what constitutes an "agreement" under existing law. Machine-learning tools for cartel screening and document review are already in use at several competition authorities, raising questions about transparency, reproducibility, and the "black box" problem in adversarial proceedings. The empirical methods for detecting and measuring algorithmic effects are still developing, but the demand-side tools from [Chapter 4: IO Toolkit](04-io-toolkit.md) and the causal methods from [Chapter 2: Research Design](02-research-design.md) provide a foundation. See @stigler_committee_2019 for early analysis of digital platform competition and the role of data and algorithms in market power.

**Labor market enforcement.** As documented in [Chapter 10: Labor Markets](10-labor-markets.md), labor antitrust has moved from the periphery to the center of enforcement priorities. Criminal wage-fixing prosecutions, civil challenges to no-poach agreements, and the FTC's attempted noncompete ban (enjoined in *Ryan LLC v. FTC* but likely to re-emerge in some form) have established that employer conduct in labor markets is subject to the same antitrust scrutiny as product-market conduct. The empirical toolkit --- labor market HHI, supply elasticity estimation, diff-in-diff designs exploiting policy variation --- is directly transferable from the product-market methods developed throughout this book. The conceptual challenge is communicating monopsony to courts and juries accustomed to thinking about consumer harm in terms of price increases rather than wage suppression.

**Digital markets and platform regulation.** The EU's Digital Markets Act, the UK's Digital Markets Unit, and similar initiatives worldwide represent a shift from ex post antitrust enforcement to ex ante platform regulation. The empirical questions --- how to measure multi-sided market power, how to assess the competitive effects of self-preferencing and data aggregation, how to design interoperability remedies --- are at the frontier of the field. The methods in [Chapter 9: Digital Markets](09-digital-markets.md) provide a starting point, but the regulatory framework is evolving rapidly. Practitioners should expect that the boundary between competition policy and sectoral regulation will continue to blur, particularly in markets where a small number of platforms serve as gatekeepers for entire ecosystems.

## A note on intellectual honesty

One theme has run through every chapter: honest communication of uncertainty is what distinguishes expert analysis from advocacy. This is not a methodological fashion but an epistemic commitment. Confidence intervals should be reported, not suppressed. Sensitivity to specification choices should be documented, not concealed. The limits of available data should be acknowledged, not papered over. Courts and tribunals are increasingly sophisticated consumers of empirical evidence, and they are rightly skeptical of analyses that present point estimates without uncertainty, that cherry-pick specifications, or that ignore inconvenient data. The best expert witnesses are those who can explain not only what the evidence shows but also what it does not show --- and why.

This commitment to intellectual honesty extends to the choice of methods themselves. There is no single "correct" approach to any antitrust question. Difference-in-differences may be the right design for one merger and entirely inappropriate for another. Structural models may be essential for prospective merger simulation but overkill for a retrospective cartel damages analysis. Synthetic control may provide the most transparent counterfactual for a single-market case but add little when a rich panel of comparable markets is available. The practitioner's judgment --- informed by the evidence triad, grounded in institutional knowledge, and tested against the standards of the relevant jurisdiction --- is irreplaceable.

## Looking ahead

The antitrust economics of the 1960s would be barely recognizable to a modern practitioner. The tools of the 2020s --- staggered DiD estimators, synthetic control, machine-learning screens, matched employer-employee data, platform telemetry --- would seem like science fiction to the economists who testified in *United States v. Aluminum Co. of America* or *Brown Shoe Co. v. United States*. And the methods of the 2040s will no doubt look incomplete to analysts working a generation from now. That is as it should be. The aspiration is not to reach a final, settled methodology but to keep refining our tools so that competition enforcement rests on the best available evidence, interpreted with intellectual honesty and communicated with clarity.

The challenges ahead are significant. Global platforms operate across jurisdictions with divergent regulatory frameworks, creating enforcement gaps and forum-shopping opportunities. AI-mediated pricing and matching algorithms may facilitate coordination that falls short of traditional legal definitions of conspiracy. Labor market concentration in the gig economy raises questions about the boundary between employee and independent contractor that antitrust law was not designed to address. Climate policy and competition policy are increasingly intertwined, as authorities grapple with the tension between sustainability agreements and cartel prohibitions. These are not problems that any single method or jurisdiction can solve.

What this book has aimed to provide is not a set of definitive answers but a toolkit --- and, more importantly, the judgment to know which tool to use when. The evidence triad, the tri-jurisdictional perspective, the workflow orientation, and the commitment to reproducible, transparent analysis are the foundations on which practitioners can build. If this book has equipped its readers to contribute to the ongoing project of making competition enforcement more rigorous, more accessible, and more responsive to the realities of modern markets, it has served its purpose.

## Reflection questions

1. **Evidence triad synthesis.** Choose a recent antitrust case from any of the three jurisdictions covered in this book. Identify the quantitative evidence, the documentary evidence, and the role of expert judgment in the outcome. How would the analysis have differed if one leg of the triad had been missing?

2. **Cross-jurisdictional translation.** Take a method from this book --- for example, difference-in-differences for merger retrospectives or HHI screening for labor markets --- and consider how its application would differ in the US, EU, and South Africa. What institutional, legal, or data constraints would shape your approach in each jurisdiction?

3. **Emerging frontiers.** Pick one of the developments discussed above --- AI and algorithmic competition, labor market enforcement, or digital platform regulation --- and sketch a research design for investigating a concrete question in that area. What data would you need? What identification strategy would you use? What qualitative evidence would complement your quantitative analysis?

4. **Intellectual honesty.** Think of a situation in which you might be tempted to present an empirical result more confidently than the evidence warrants. What safeguards --- pre-analysis plans, robustness checks, peer review, disclosure of limitations --- would help you maintain intellectual honesty? How would you communicate uncertainty to a non-technical audience such as a judge or jury?

5. **Your contribution.** The field of antitrust methods is evolving. What gap in the current toolkit do you see most clearly? What method, data source, or analytical approach would you most like to develop or improve? How does the foundation in this book position you to make that contribution?
