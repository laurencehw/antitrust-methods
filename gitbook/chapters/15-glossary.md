# Glossary and Reference Guide {.unnumbered}

This glossary collects key terms, statutes, cases, and methods referenced throughout the book. It is designed as a quick-reference tool for practitioners and students navigating the tri-jurisdictional material.

## Key Terms

**AEC test (As-Efficient-Competitor test).** A test used in margin squeeze and predatory pricing cases to determine whether a hypothetical competitor as efficient as the dominant firm could profitably compete at the prices charged. Used in both EU and South African abuse-of-dominance analysis.

**Bargaining leverage.** The relative power of parties in bilateral negotiations, determined by their disagreement payoffs (outside options). Central to Nash-in-Nash models of hospital-insurer and platform-merchant negotiations. See (sec)-bargaining.

**Before/after method.** A cartel damages approach that compares prices during the cartel period to prices in a competitive "before" or "after" period. Requires structural break tests to validate the competitive benchmark. See [Chapter 5](chapters/05-cartels.md).

**Critical loss analysis.** A market definition tool that asks how many sales a hypothetical monopolist could afford to lose before a SSNIP (Small but Significant and Non-transitory Increase in Price) becomes unprofitable. See [Chapter 3](chapters/03-market-definition.md).

**Diversion ratio.** The fraction of sales lost by one product that are captured by another product when the first product's price increases. Core input for UPP/GUPPI calculations and merger simulation.

**Essential facilities doctrine.** The principle that a dominant firm controlling infrastructure essential for competition (e.g., a port, pipeline, or telecommunications network) may have a duty to provide access to competitors. Recognized in EU law; more limited in US law after *Trinko*.

**Evidence triad.** The book's organizing framework: the integration of empirical analysis (quantitative estimation), documentary evidence (qualitative context), and expert judgment (translation to legal standards).

**GUPPI (Gross Upward Pricing Pressure Index).** A merger screening metric equal to the diversion ratio from one merging product to the other, multiplied by the other product's margin. Expressed as a percentage of price; values above 5% typically warrant closer scrutiny.

**HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index).** The sum of squared market shares (multiplied by 10,000) for all firms in a market. The primary concentration measure in US, EU, and South African merger guidelines. Thresholds: below 1,500 (unconcentrated), 1,500--2,500 (moderately concentrated), above 2,500 (highly concentrated).

**Labor supply elasticity.** The responsiveness of employment to wage changes at the firm level. Elasticities below 2--3 indicate meaningful monopsony power. See [Chapter 10](chapters/10-labor-markets.md).

**Monopsony.** Buyer-side market power: the ability of an employer to suppress wages below the competitive level. The labor-market analogue to monopoly power. See [Chapter 10](chapters/10-labor-markets.md).

**Nash-in-Nash.** A bargaining model in which each upstream-downstream pair simultaneously engages in bilateral Nash bargaining. Used to predict negotiated price changes from hospital mergers and other structural changes. See (sec)-bargaining.

**SSNIP (Small but Significant and Non-transitory Increase in Price).** A hypothetical price increase (typically 5--10%) used to test market definition: if a hypothetical monopolist could profitably impose a SSNIP, the candidate market is a relevant antitrust market.

**Synthetic control.** A causal inference method that constructs a weighted combination of untreated units to match a treated unit's pre-treatment trajectory. Used for single-unit treatment effects such as one-off mergers or regulatory interventions. See [Chapter 2](chapters/02-research-design.md).

**UPP (Upward Pricing Pressure).** A merger screening framework that estimates the incentive for a merged firm to raise the price of one product, based on diversion ratios and margins of the other merging product. Does not require full market definition.

**Willingness-to-pay (WTP) framework.** A hospital merger analysis method that measures how much an insurer's enrollees value having a particular hospital in-network, used to assess bargaining leverage.

## Statutes and Guidelines

### United States
- **Sherman Act (1890).** Sections 1 and 2: prohibition on conspiracies in restraint of trade and monopolization.
- **Clayton Act (1914).** Section 7: prohibition on mergers that "may be substantially to lessen competition." Section 6: statutory labor exemption.
- **FTC Act (1914).** Section 5: prohibition on unfair methods of competition.
- **Norris-LaGuardia Act (1932).** Shields collective bargaining and union activities from antitrust.
- **2023 Merger Guidelines.** DOJ/FTC joint guidelines establishing six theories of harm and updated HHI thresholds. (DOJ/FTC Merger Guidelines, 2023).
- **2010 Horizontal Merger Guidelines.** Prior guidelines; still referenced in case law. (DOJ/FTC Horizontal Merger Guidelines, 2010).

### European Union
- **TFEU Article 101.** Prohibition on anti-competitive agreements (cartels, vertical restraints).
- **TFEU Article 102.** Prohibition on abuse of dominant position.
- **EU Merger Regulation (139/2004).** SIEC test: "significant impediment to effective competition."
- **2004 Horizontal Merger Guidelines.** (EC Horizontal Merger Guidelines, 2004).
- **Digital Markets Act (2022/1925).** Ex ante regulation of gatekeeper platforms. (Digital Markets Act, 2022).

### South Africa
- **Competition Act 89 of 1998.** Prohibits restrictive practices (Chapter 2), abuse of dominance (Chapter 3), and regulates mergers (Chapter 4). Includes public-interest factors. (SA Competition Act, 1998).
- **2023 Merger Guidelines.** Competition Commission guidelines on merger assessment. (Sa Merger Guidelines, 2023).
- **Competition Amendment Act 18 of 2018.** Expanded abuse-of-dominance provisions to address buyer power, price discrimination, and refusal to deal.

## Key Cases

### United States
- *United States v. Aluminum Co. of America* (Alcoa), 148 F.2d 416 (2d Cir. 1945). Market definition and monopoly power.
- *Brown Shoe Co. v. United States*, 370 U.S. 294 (1962). Merger enforcement and market definition.
- *United States v. Microsoft Corp.*, 253 F.3d 34 (D.C. Cir. 2001). Platform monopolization and tying. (*United States v. Microsoft*, 2001).
- *Ohio v. American Express Co.*, 585 U.S. 529 (2018). Two-sided market definition. (*Ohio v. American Express*, 2018).
- *United States v. Google LLC* (Search), No. 1:20-cv-03010 (D.D.C. 2024). Digital platform monopolization. (*United States v. Google (Search)*, 2023).
- *United States v. JetBlue Airways Corp. & Spirit Airlines*, No. 1:22-cv-02439 (D.D.C. 2023). Airline merger blocked. (Us Spirit Jetblue, 2023).
- *United States v. DaVita Inc. and Kent Thiry*, No. 1:21-CR-00229 (D. Colo. 2022). Criminal no-poach prosecution. (*United States v. DaVita*, 2022).
- *Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc.*, 509 U.S. 579 (1993). Expert testimony admissibility. (Daubert, 1993).

### European Union
- *European Commission v. Microsoft*, Case T-201/04 (2007). Tying and interoperability.
- *Intel v. Commission*, Case C-413/14 P (2017). Rebates and as-efficient-competitor test.
- *Google Shopping*, Case AT.39740 (2017). Self-preferencing and platform dominance.

### South Africa
- *Competition Commission v. Pioneer Food (Pty) Ltd et al.* (Bread Cartel), Tribunal Case 15/CR/Feb07 (2007). (Sa Bread Cartel, 2007).
- *Competition Commission v. Sasol Chemical Industries* (Polypropylene), Tribunal Case 107/CR/Sep11 (2014). (*Competition Commission v. Sasol*, 2014).
- *Competition Commission v. Telkom SA SOC Ltd*, Tribunal Case 60/CR/Oct12 (2013). (*Competition Commission v. Telkom*, 2013).
- *Walmart/Massmart*, Tribunal Case 127/CR/Sep10 (2011). Merger with public-interest conditions.
- *Mediclinic/Matlosana*, Tribunal Case 94/LM/Nov14 (2014). Prohibited hospital merger.
- *Heineken/Distell/Capevin*, Tribunal Case 133/CR/Sep21 (2022). Conditional approval with investment commitments.

## Additional Terms

**Cellophane fallacy.** The error of defining a market too broadly by using the prevailing (monopoly) price as the benchmark for substitution analysis. Named after *United States v. E.I. du Pont de Nemours & Co.* (1956), where the Supreme Court found that cellophane competed with other flexible wrapping materials, ignoring that du Pont's monopoly pricing had already made substitutes appear competitive. Relevant to SSNIP analysis: the hypothetical monopolist test should start from the competitive price, not the current price.

**ECPR (Efficient Component Pricing Rule).** An access pricing formula that sets the wholesale charge equal to the incumbent's incremental cost of providing access plus the opportunity cost of lost retail sales. Also known as the Baumol-Willig rule. Used in telecommunications and rail access remedies. See [Chapter 8](chapters/08-regulation-remedies.md).

**Per se vs. rule of reason.** Two standards of antitrust analysis. *Per se* illegality applies to conduct that is presumed anticompetitive without detailed economic analysis (e.g., horizontal price-fixing, bid-rigging). The *rule of reason* requires a full economic analysis of the conduct's competitive effects, weighing pro-competitive justifications against anti-competitive harms. Most conduct---including vertical restraints, most mergers, and unilateral conduct---is evaluated under the rule of reason. South Africa uses a similar distinction, with certain horizontal agreements subject to a "per se prohibition" under Section 4(1)(a) of the Competition Act.

**Public interest test.** A feature of South African merger review (and some other emerging-economy regimes) that requires the Competition Tribunal to consider factors beyond competitive effects, including employment impacts, small business participation, the ability of historically disadvantaged persons to participate in the economy, and the effect on specific industries or regions. No direct analogue in US or EU merger law.

**SIEC test (Significant Impediment to Effective Competition).** The EU's substantive test for merger review under the EU Merger Regulation. Broader than the US "substantial lessening of competition" (SLC) test in that it explicitly captures non-coordinated effects in oligopolistic markets (the "gap" created by the removal of a maverick firm) without requiring proof of collective dominance.

**SSNDQ (Small but Significant Non-transitory Decrease in Quality).** The zero-price analogue of SSNIP, used to define markets where users pay with attention or data rather than money. Asks whether a hypothetical monopolist could profitably degrade quality (e.g., increase ad load, reduce privacy protections) by a small but significant amount. Used in digital market investigations.

**Trinko doctrine.** The US Supreme Court's holding in *Verizon v. Trinko* (2004) that there is generally no duty to deal with competitors under Section 2 of the Sherman Act, narrowing the essential facilities doctrine. Contrasts with the EU's more expansive approach under (*Bronner*, 1998) and (*IMS Health*, 2004).
